# Algorithms, fairness, and due process in NYC parking enforcement

The research converges on a single thesis: **automated and algorithmic decision-making in administrative enforcement regimes — from NYC parking adjudication to bail and policing — routinely amplifies pre-existing inequities while eroding classical due-process protections, and the current generation of legal AI tools is not yet reliable enough to close the gap**. The NYC parking system is a revealing case study because it processes 9–11 million tickets annually, dismisses roughly 30% at hearing, relies on enforcement data contaminated by selection bias, and is increasingly being augmented by machine-learning tools that inherit the classical fairness-accuracy trade-offs documented in the algorithmic-fairness literature. Foundational work by Citron (2008), Barocas and Selbst (2016), Hardt et al. (2016), Kleinberg et al. (2016), and Chouldechova (2017) provides the conceptual spine; empirical studies from Stanford's Open Policing Project, Stanford RegLab, Streetsblog, and the NYC Comptroller supply the ground truth. Together they define both the promise and the outer limits of deploying predictive models in this domain.

## What the NYC parking data actually shows

New York City's enforcement regime is **enormous, financially material, and highly contested**. Parking fines alone generated **$545 million in FY2016** and parking tickets represent roughly 55% of total city fines revenue, with 85,000 metered spaces feeding 9–11 million tickets per year (NYC Comptroller). In FY2022, **1.7 million tickets totaling $133 million** went to hearing; administrative law judges dismissed **29.76% (506,011 tickets, $36.1 million)** on not-guilty findings, and of the small fraction appealed (24,132), another **20.2% were dismissed**. In the adjacent OATH (non-parking) adjudication system, analysis of 2.15 million cases showed **roughly 64% of violations were ultimately dismissed or written off** — a staggering error or discretion rate that signals systemic issuing-side problems, not merely defendant success.

Enforcement itself is deeply uneven. Streetsblog's analyses document that **only four of 77 precincts account for nearly half of unsubstantiated 311-complaint summonses**, that **just 1.9% of 76,000 blocked bike-lane complaints (2016–2023) produced tickets**, and that 4,817 vehicles already in judgment received fresh tickets during a 90-day window because traffic-enforcement agents cannot see the "in-judgment" database — a classic case of **technological due process failure in Citron's sense**: code, not policy, is silently determining outcomes. The top scofflaw owed $114,000 across 913 tickets concentrated in a single precinct. A 2024 Streetsblog/NYU study using AI-monitored cameras found a **311 ticket-issuance rate of 11.21%**, with NYPD closures frequently contradicted by video evidence.

**The core implication: the training data any ML system would use — tickets issued, dismissals, 311 outcomes — is selectively labeled and spatially biased.** Lakkaraju et al.'s 2017 KDD paper on the **selective labels problem** formalizes exactly this trap: we observe outcomes only for the subset of cases human decision-makers chose to advance, making naive accuracy comparisons between human adjudicators and algorithms systematically misleading. In parking terms, we never observe the counterfactual dismissal rate for tickets that were never issued, never contested, or written off administratively.

## The classical due-process critique still governs

**Danielle Citron's "Technological Due Process" (85 Wash. U. L. Rev. 1249, 2008) remains the controlling theoretical frame.** Citron argued that automated administrative systems collapse adjudication and rulemaking while honoring the procedural safeguards of neither: hearings become "devalued by the lack of meaningful notice and by the hearing officer's tendency to presume a computer system's infallibility," and the *Mathews v. Eldridge* cost-benefit test cannot rationally compare the high fixed cost of auditing code against the diffuse benefit of catching many small errors. Programmers, not legislators, end up writing substantive rules when they translate statutes into code. This diagnosis maps almost perfectly onto NYC's hearing-by-mail system, the Pay-by-Plate placard project, and any future AI-assisted adjudication: **the transparency deficit is structural, not incidental**.

Barocas and Selbst's "Big Data's Disparate Impact" (104 Cal. L. Rev. 671, 2016) extends the argument to substantive anti-discrimination law. Because data-mined models "inherit the prejudices of prior decision makers" and Title VII's business-necessity defense readily accommodates predictive correlations, **disparate-impact doctrine is a weak remedy against algorithmic bias**. For NYC parking — where ticketing patterns reflect decades of discretionary precinct-level policing — any model trained on historical issuance will encode those patterns as "ground truth."

## The fairness-accuracy impossibility results

Three papers establish that **one cannot simultaneously satisfy the intuitive fairness criteria** most stakeholders would want from an automated ticket-contest or enforcement-allocation system.

Hardt, Price, and Srebro's **"Equality of Opportunity in Supervised Learning" (NeurIPS 2016)** formalized *equalized odds* and *equal opportunity* — requiring equal true-positive (and sometimes false-positive) rates across protected groups — and showed how to post-process any classifier to satisfy them by shifting the burden of error from disadvantaged groups to the decision-maker. Kleinberg, Mullainathan, and Raghavan's **"Inherent Trade-Offs in the Fair Determination of Risk Scores" (ITCS 2017, arXiv:1609.05807)** proved that **calibration, balance for the positive class, and balance for the negative class cannot all be satisfied simultaneously except in degenerate cases where base rates are equal or prediction is perfect**. Chouldechova's **"Fair Prediction with Disparate Impact" (Big Data 5:2, 2017)** demonstrated the same incompatibility for recidivism tools and showed how failure of error-rate balance translates directly into disparate impact when group prevalence differs.

The practical consequence for NYC: **if ticketing rates or dismissal rates differ by neighborhood demographics (they do), any model will force a policy choice among incompatible fairness definitions**. There is no technically neutral default.

## Empirical evidence of bias in policing data

Pierson et al.'s **"A large-scale analysis of racial disparities in police stops"** (Nature Human Behaviour 4:736, 2020), analyzing **nearly 100 million traffic stops**, provides the strongest empirical baseline. Using the "veil of darkness" test, the authors found Black drivers were less likely to be stopped after sunset — evidence of bias in the stop decision itself — and that **search thresholds for Black and Hispanic drivers were systematically lower than for white drivers**, a pattern that persisted even after recreational-marijuana legalization reduced overall search volumes. Lum and Isaac's **"To predict and serve?" (Significance 13:14, 2016)** showed that PredPol-style predictive policing, fed drug-arrest data from Oakland, would direct officers disproportionately to Black neighborhoods even though public-health surveys indicated drug use was geographically uniform — the canonical demonstration that **"police data" measures enforcement, not crime**.

For parking specifically, this means models built on NYC ticket issuance would re-inscribe the precinct-level discretionary patterns Streetsblog has documented: protected placards going unticketed near station houses, aggressive enforcement in some neighborhoods, and near-zero placard enforcement in others.

## The machine-learning toolkit: strong on tabular data, weak on legal text

The empirical case for **tree-based gradient boosting** on the kind of structured ticket data NYC maintains is now very strong. Grinsztajn, Oyallon, and Varoquaux's **"Why do tree-based models still outperform deep learning on typical tabular data?" (NeurIPS 2022 Datasets & Benchmarks, arXiv:2207.08815)** benchmarked 45 datasets and found **tree-based models remain state-of-the-art at ~10K-sample scale** because neural networks struggle with uninformative features, rotational non-invariance, and irregular functions. Prokhorenkova et al.'s **CatBoost (NeurIPS 2018, arXiv:1706.09516)** introduced *ordered boosting* and a principled encoding of categorical features that eliminates the target-leakage prediction shift present in all prior gradient-boosting implementations — particularly valuable for parking data rich in high-cardinality categoricals (violation code, precinct, vehicle make, plate type).

Legal *text* is a harder problem. Aletras et al.'s **"Predicting judicial decisions of the European Court of Human Rights" (PeerJ CS 2:e93, 2016)** achieved 79% average accuracy predicting Article-3/6/8 violations from case text alone using N-grams and topic clusters; the "facts" section was the dominant predictor, a result the authors framed as empirical support for legal realism. But subsequent replication (Medvedeva et al. 2019) showed accuracy drops to 58–68% when predicting genuinely future cases — a warning that **backward-looking benchmark accuracy overstates operational utility**.

The most important recent finding for any legal-AI deployment is the **hallucination problem**. Dahl, Magesh, Suzgun, and Ho's **"Large Legal Fictions" (J. Legal Analysis 16:64, 2024)** showed general-purpose LLMs hallucinate on legal queries **between 58% (GPT-4) and 88% (Llama 2)** of the time when asked verifiable questions about random federal cases, and — more troublingly — models often fail to correct users' false legal premises. The follow-up **"Hallucination-Free? Assessing the Reliability of Leading AI Legal Research Tools"** (Magesh et al., J. Empirical Legal Studies 22:216, 2025) tested RAG-based commercial products and found **Lexis+ AI and Ask Practical Law AI hallucinated on 17%+ of queries, and Westlaw AI-Assisted Research on ~33%**, despite vendor claims of "hallucination-free" output. The LegalBench project (Guha et al., NeurIPS 2023, arXiv:2308.11462), a 162-task collaborative benchmark, confirms that legal-reasoning performance varies sharply by task type — some IRAC-style tasks are within reach, others are far beyond current models.

**The synthesis: for structured ticket-status prediction, boosted trees are production-ready; for natural-language legal argumentation (drafting a contest, interpreting a statute), no current system clears the reliability bar a due-process-sensitive system would demand.**

## What a responsible system would look like

Drawing together the threads, a defensible NYC parking-ticket analytics or contest system would need five properties, each traceable to the literature above. First, it must treat **training data as selectively labeled** (Lakkaraju 2017) and avoid claiming neutrality for models trained on ticket-issuance patterns that reflect precinct discretion (Streetsblog, Pierson 2020, Lum & Isaac 2016). Second, it must make an **explicit fairness choice** — equalized odds, calibration, or demographic parity — knowing the others cannot be satisfied jointly (Kleinberg 2016, Chouldechova 2017, Hardt 2016). Third, it must **publish its code logic and surrogate rules** to satisfy Citron's technological-due-process standard, not merely its outputs. Fourth, for tabular prediction it should use **CatBoost or similar boosted-tree methods** rather than deep learning at NYC's data scale (Grinsztajn 2022, Prokhorenkova 2018). Fifth, any LLM-generated legal text — whether for hearing preparation or summons interpretation — must be **gated by human verification**, given documented hallucination rates of 17–88% across tools (Dahl 2024, Magesh 2025).

## Conclusion

The literature tells a consistent story that the NYC parking data illustrates vividly: **administrative enforcement is already partially automated, already biased, and already inadequately supervised**, and the tools now being proposed to "fix" it — predictive allocation, automated adjudication, LLM-drafted decisions — bring mathematically provable fairness trade-offs and empirically documented reliability failures. The most important insight is that the choice is not between fair human judgment and biased machines; it is between **opaque discretion and explicit, auditable trade-offs**. The value of an algorithmic system in this domain lies less in raw accuracy gains than in forcing previously invisible enforcement policies — which blocks get ticketed, which complaints get dismissed, which placards get ignored — into the open where they can be contested as rules rather than suffered as code. Citron's 2008 prescription, nearly two decades old, reads almost as a direct response to the 2024 NYC parking-enforcement scandals: the procedural reforms we need are the ones that make the machine's logic reviewable by the same democratic processes that governed the rules it replaced.
